Phase 2 — Post-Turnover / Rental

After the building is finished and you receive your unit. This is when rental income starts (if applicable).
If you're taking a bank loan for the remaining balance, how many years will you pay it off? Enter 0 if you're paying in full cash at turnover.
The annual interest rate on your bank loan. This is what the bank charges you for borrowing money. Enter 0 if you're paying cash — no loan needed.
How much rent you expect to collect per month if you rent out the unit. Check similar units in the area for a realistic number. Enter 0 if you plan to live in it.
What percentage of the year do you expect the unit to be rented? 90% means about 11 months rented, 1 month vacant (for tenant turnover, cleaning, etc.).
The monthly fee you pay to the building management for shared facilities — security, pool, gym, maintenance, elevators, common areas. Every condo charges this.
Annual real property tax and other yearly costs like insurance. This is a yearly amount, not monthly.

Projection Assumptions

These are educated estimates about the future. No one can predict exactly, but historical data gives us reasonable ranges.
How much the property's value is expected to increase each year. If a ₱10M property appreciates 8% per year, it's worth ₱10.8M after Year 1. This compounds over time.
How much the rent increases each year. Most leases allow a small annual increase — typically 3-5%. This means your rental income grows over time.
How far into the future you want to project. 10 years is a common horizon for real estate investments. This is the point at which we calculate your total returns.
The costs of selling the property later: Capital Gains Tax (6% in the Philippines), broker commission (typically 3%), and miscellaneous fees. These are deducted from your sale price when calculating profit.

The expected annual return from stocks or REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts — companies that own income-producing properties and trade like stocks). Historically 8-12% per year, but with higher risk.

Key Investment Metrics

Total ROI
—
Net of all costs
Return on Investment — how much profit you made compared to how much you spent. If you invested ₱2M and earned ₱1M profit, your ROI is 50%.
Annualized Return
—
Per year (CAGR)
Your average yearly return, compounded. Think of it as "if my money grew at a steady rate each year, what would that rate be?" This is the most useful number for comparing against bank deposits or stocks.
Gross Rental Yield
—
At turnover rent
Your annual rental income as a percentage of the property price — before deducting any expenses. Higher is better. Tells you how much income the property generates relative to what it cost.
Net Rental Yield
—
After dues + tax
Same as gross yield, but after subtracting association dues and property tax. This is your real, take-home rental return. This is the number that matters for cash flow.
Property Value at Exit
—
—
What the property is estimated to be worth when you decide to sell. This is based on the annual appreciation rate you set — the property's value grows each year like compound interest.
Total Capital Appreciation
—
—
How much the property's value has increased from your purchase price to the exit year. If you bought at ₱10M and it's now worth ₱18M, that's 80% total appreciation.

Every month that passes, three things happen simultaneously: the property price adjusts upward, rental income you could have been collecting is missed, and the pre-selling advantage narrows. This tool quantifies those three forces using the same numbers you entered in your analysis.
